Abstract 
In today’s competitive job market, many job seekers struggle to understand how well their CVs align 
with job descriptions and often receive little or no meaningful feedback after submitting 
applications. At the same time, recruiters face the challenge of reviewing large volumes of 
applications while identifying candidates who best fit the role. Although intelligent technologies are 
increasingly used in recruitment, many existing systems prioritise automation for recruiters rather 
than providing support and feedback to job seekers. 
This project develops an AI Career Advisor web application that supports both job seekers and 
recruiters by analysing CVs and job postings to provide matching results and explainable insights. 
The system uses text processing and embedding-based similarity methods to extract relevant 
information, particularly skills, from CVs and job descriptions. These are stored in FAISS vector 
indexes, enabling efficient similarity retrieval. Based on this, the system recommends relevant job 
opportunities to job seekers and presents recruiters with ranked candidate matches through an 
interactive dashboard, helping to reduce manual screening effort. 
The prototype integrates a FastAPI backend with a Streamlit frontend, providing two dedicated user 
portals. A hybrid matching approach that combines semantic similarity, skill overlap, and analysis of 
missing skills is used to improve match quality and provide transparent feedback. 
Evaluation of the system using controlled test cases and dataset samples showed that the hybrid 
approach produces more balanced and accurate results compared to keyword-based and TF-IDF 
methods. The system also delivered consistent outputs and improved transparency through 
explanation features, including matching and missing skills.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1. Project Background 
Finding suitable employment has become increasingly difficult in recent years as competition within 
the job market continues to rise. Many job seekers now rely on online platforms such as LinkedIn, 
Indeed and Glassdoor, yet still experience frustration due to unclear job requirements, poor 
feedback and limited understanding of how well their CVs match advertised roles. Recruiters, on the 
other hand, face the challenge of screening large volumes of applications efficiently and consistently 
while avoiding bias. 
During my internship at SAP, I gained firsthand insight into how complex and time-consuming the 
recruitment process can be in large organisations. From drafting job postings and screening 
applications to conducting multiple interview rounds and extending final offers, I observed the many 
manual steps and inefficiencies that exist even in highly digital environments. This experience 
highlighted that Artificial Intelligence (AI) is increasingly necessary to streamline the hiring process, 
reduce recruiter workload and provide a more transparent and supportive experience for 
candidates. 
As a final year computer science student, job searching is a significant and often uncertain challenge. 
This reinforced the need for a system that not only matches candidates to roles but also provides 
clear and meaningful feedback. These combined experiences motivated the development of a 
solution that supports both sides of the recruitment process.  
AI and Natural Language Processing (NLP) are increasingly being applied across industries, including 
recruitment. These technologies can analyse and interpret text-based data such as CVs and job 
descriptions to identify patterns and extract relevant information. A recent review of 49 studies 
found that while AI tools in recruitment can boost productivity and automate screening, they also 
raise critical questions of transparency and fairness. (Dadaboyev, 2025). 
The AI Career Advisor project aims to address these challenges by developing a dual-portal web 
application that supports both job seekers and recruiters. Job seekers receive insights into how their 
CVs align with job postings, while recruiters can upload vacancies and view ranked candidate 
matches. This system focuses on improving feedback, transparency and efficiency within the 
recruitment process. 
 
1.2. Project Description 
The AI Career Advisor is a web-based system built using a FastAPI backend and a Streamlit frontend. 
It supports two main user portals: a Job Seeker Portal and a Recruiter Portal. When a CV or job 
posting is uploaded, the text is cleaned and processed, relevant skills are identified, and embeddings 
are generated. These embeddings are stored in separate FAISS vector indexes for job postings and 
resumes, allowing efficient similarity retrieval between candidate and job data. 
For job seekers, the system compares CVs to job postings and returns matched roles, along with final 
match scores, identified skill gaps, and feedback on how well the CV aligns with each role. For 
recruiters, the system compares job postings against resumes and returns a ranked list of candidate 
matches.

2. Literature Review 
 
2.1. Introduction 
 
This chapter critically examines existing systems, tools and technologies related to AI-driven 
recruitment and recommendation systems. It evaluates how current platforms operate, identifies 
key limitations and highlights gaps that motivate the development of the AI Career Advisor. The 
review focuses on NLP, recommendation systems, and data visualisation approaches, as well as on 
selected final-year projects that influenced the design of this system. 
 
2.2. Alternative Existing Solutions  
 
In recent years, AI has reshaped recruitment by helping employers automate parts of the hiring 
process, such as CV screening, skill matching and communication analysis. Many large companies 
now rely on AI-powered recruitment to manage high volumes of applicants. According to LinkedIn, 
83% of organisations now use some form of AI within their recruitment pipeline, primarily to 
improve efficiency and quality of hire. While this automation benefits employers, studies show 
limited transparency for candidates, who often remain unaware of why they were shortlisted or 
rejected. (LinkedIn , 2025). 
Platforms such as Eightfold AI, HiringBranch, Seekout and Sapia.ai represent the forefront of AI-
powered recruitment. Eightfold’s approach to automated skill extraction is particularly notable. (AI 
talent acquisition & recruiting platform, n.d.), while HiringBranch’s use of interactive dashboards 
influenced the design of the recruiter dashboard in this project to make decision-making seamless 
based on candidate performance and skill fit (A Guide to Artificial Intelligence Resume Screening, 
n.d.) SeekOut applies AI analytics for diversity-focused talent searches. (Agentic AI Talent Platform 
for Smarter Hiring, n.d.) Sapia.ai provides bias-reduced structured interviews through NLP. (AI 
Career Coach, n.d.). 
Workday introduced its Recruiting Agent, which automates outreach and candidate matching, 
improving recruiter capacity by 54% (Ferrazzi, 2025). Mantrika uses machine learning to predict 
candidate performance, removing many manual recruitment steps. (RecruitEdge | Mantrika.ai, 
2025). Fountain’s AI demonstrates helping both recruiters and candidates. The Hiring Assistant 
automates screening for high-volume roles and supports multilingual interviews, improving speed 
and fairness in hiring, reducing hiring costs up to 50% (Fountain AI Recruiter, n.d.). While the 
Candidate AI Agent gives applicants instant answers, personalised guidance, and around-the-clock 
support, leading to a 50% reduction in support tickets (Candidate AI Agent, n.d.). (Ferrazzi, 2025) 
However, a consistent gap across these systems indicates they are built primarily for employers, 
offering little visibility or transparency to applicants. AI Career Advisor differentiates itself by 
introducing transparency through two dedicated portals, one for recruiters and one for job seekers, 
providing clear explanations behind each recommendation and feedback on how applicants’ skills 
align with job postings. 
While these platforms demonstrate the effectiveness of AI in improving recruitment efficiency, they 
are primarily designed to support recruiters rather than candidates. As a result, they provide limited

2.3. Technologies Researched 
To support the project’s dual-portal architecture, several technologies were researched for 
efficiency, scalability and accessibility. 
The backend was implemented using FastAPI, a high-performance Python framework. (FastAPI, n.d.). 
Other frameworks were considered, such as Flask and Django, but FastAPI’s native support for 
Pydantic models and its automatic documentation made it ideal for API development. 
The frontend was built in Streamlit, chosen for its ability to prototype accessible, interactive web 
applications without requiring advanced frontend coding. (Streamlit • A faster way to build and 
share data apps, 2021)  
For data management, SQLite was used during initial development for simplicity. A scalable 
alternative would be PostgreSQL for future development. 
AI-based matching relied on FAISS for vector storage and retrieval, enabling semantic-level 
comparison of CV embeddings and job descriptions. (Meta, n.d.)Text embeddings were generated 
using a lightweight embedding approach, allowing CVs and job descriptions to be represented 
numerically for similarity comparison, a common technique in natural language processing. (Tomas 
Mikolov, 2013) FAISS was used to efficiently retrieve vectors, enabling semantic matching beyond 
simple keyword overlap. These technologies were selected not only for their technical capabilities 
but also for their ability to support a transparent, explainable AI pipeline.

2.4. Other Relevant Research  
 
While AI adoption in recruitment is growing drastically, its integration into Applicant Tracking 
Systems (ATS) has revealed several ethical issues. Traditional ATS solutions use AI for keyword-based 
matching, which is an approach criticised for lacking transparency and nuance. For example, a 
candidate may be rejected if their CV lacks specific keywords, even if they have the desired 
experience. This reliance on predefined keywords results in many qualified applicants being 
overlooked. The opacity of these systems contributes to candidates’ frustration; a CareerBuilder 
survey found that 75% of candidates never hear back from employers after applying, underscoring 
how ATSs can harm communication. (Sameer, 2024) (Applicant Tracking Systems: Everything You 
Need to Know, n.d.) 
These challenges underscore the need for fairness and transparency. Organisations should prioritise 
explainable AI, feedback loops and human oversights to balance automation with empathy. Design 
clarity and transparency also influence the perceived trustworthiness of data use and ethical 
compliance in AI systems. Findings show that aligning user expectations with visible system 
behaviour builds long-term confidence. (Glassberg, 2025) This research influenced AI Career 
Advisor’s decision to include user-friendly dashboards, visual explanations of AI matches, and 
feedback features to improve user engagement. 
Early studies also suggest that AI-driven hiring can outperform human recruiters in terms of speed, 
efficiency, and diversity. The Inclusion Initiative found that AI recruitment increased hiring efficiency 
and reduced bias compared to traditional methods. 70% of candidates valued reduced response 
times and objective evaluations from AI tools, but an equal percentage cited concerns about a lack 
of human touch and transparency (Horodyski, 2023). These insights reinforce the rationale for 
designing an AI Career Advisor with user trust and fairness as core principles. 
These findings highlight a key tension in AI recruitment systems: while efficiency improves, 
transparency and user trust often decrease. This reinforces the importance of designing systems 
that balance automation with explainability. The AI Career Advisor addresses this by prioritising clear

3. System Analysis 
 
3.1 System Overview   
The AI Career Advisor is a web-based system designed to support both job seekers and recruiters 
through a dual-portal structure. The system aims to improve efficiency and decision-making in the 
recruitment process by providing explainable job and candidate matching. 
Job seekers can upload CVs and receive matched job recommendations, along with feedback such as 
match scores and missing skills. Recruiters can upload job postings and view ranked candidate 
matches, allowing them to identify suitable applicants more efficiently. 
Unlike traditional recruitment systems that rely heavily on keyword matching or manual review, this 
system combines text processing, embedding-based retrieval, and hybrid scoring to provide more 
meaningful results. Separate FAISS vector indexes are used for job postings and resumes, supporting 
both job seeker and recruiter workflows. 
The system is designed to ensure consistency, so identical inputs produce identical outputs. This 
addresses common concerns about unreliable AI behaviour and supports user trust through 
predictable, explainable results.  
 
3.2 Requirements Gathering 
Key Stakeholders 
To ensure the system is both user-focused and covers the functional requirements, four main 
stakeholder groups were identified: 
• 
Job Seekers: 
Students, graduates and early career professionals seeking jobs. 
They require personalised career recommendations, feedback on skills and clear 
explanations of job matches. 
 
• 
Recruiters: 
Responsible for seeking out and evaluating candidates. They seek efficiency, fairness and 
tools that reduce manual workload while improving candidate quality. 
 
• 
Universities: 
Institutions that can integrate the platform into employability programmes to support 
students transitioning from education to the workplace. 
 
• 
System Administration: 
Manage system maintenance, monitor system performance and ensure consistent system 
behaviour.

4.4. Overview of System 
The AI Career Advisor is a modular, full-stack web application designed to support both job seekers 
and recruiters through a dual-portal structure. The system is built using a Streamlit frontend, a 
FastAPI backend and a data layer consisting of a relational database and FAISS vector indexes. 
The system allows job seekers to upload CVs and receive matched job recommendations, along with 
feedback such as match scores, missing skills, and skill-based insights. Recruiters can upload job 
postings and receive ranked lists of candidate matches, reducing manual screening effort and 
improving decision-making. 
The overall system is divided into three main layers: 
• 
Frontend Layer - Streamlit 
• 
Backend Layer - FastAPI 
• 
Data and Matching Layer - SQLite and FAISS 
The frontend is responsible for handling user interaction. It provides separate interfaces for job 
seekers and recruiters, allowing users to upload data, view results, and interact with the system. The 
design focuses on clarity and usability, ensuring that outputs such as match scores and feedback are 
easy to understand. 
The backend manages the core application logic and acts as the bridge between the user interface 
and the underlying processing components. It handles API requests, processes user input, 
coordinates matching workflows and returns results to the frontend. 
The matching functionality is implemented through a structured pipeline. When a CV or job posting 
is uploaded, the text is first cleaned and processed. Relevant skills and patterns are then identified, 
and embeddings are generated to represent the content numerically. These embeddings are stored 
in FAISS vector indexes, which allow efficient similarity retrieval. 
The system uses two separate FAISS indexes: 
• 
A job postings index for matching CVs to jobs (job seeker workflow) 
• 
A resume index for matching job postings to candidates (recruiter workflow) 
This separation allows the system to support bidirectional matching while keeping each workflow 
efficient and clearly defined. 
After FAISS retrieval, results are further refined using a hybrid scoring approach. This combines 
semantic similarity, skill overlap, and missing skill analysis to produce a final match score. On the 
recruiter side, an additional domain-aware re-ranking step is applied to improve the relevance of 
candidate results.

Finally, the system generates explainable outputs, including matching skills, missing skills, and 
overall scores. This ensures that users can understand how results were produced, improving 
transparency and trust in the system.

4.5 Key Design Decisions 
This section outlines the key design decisions made during the development of the AI Career Advisor 
system. These decisions were guided by the need to balance technical performance, usability, and 
transparency, while aligning with the requirements identified in Chapter 3. 
Choice of Frontend and Backend 
The system uses Streamlit for the frontend and FastAPI for the backend. Streamlit was chosen for its 
ability to rapidly develop interactive data-driven interfaces, making it suitable for building 
dashboards for both job seekers and recruiters (Streamlit • A faster way to build and share data 
apps, 2021). FastAPI was selected due to its performance, simplicity and strong support for modular 
API development. (FastAPI, n.d.) This combination allowed for a clear separation between the user 
interface and the backend logic. 
Use of FAISS for Retrieval 
FAISS was selected for efficient vector similarity search, enabling fast retrieval of relevant matches 
based on embedding similarity. Unlike traditional database queries, FAISS supports high-dimensional 
vector comparisons, making it well-suited for semantic matching between CVs and job descriptions. 
Use of Separate FAISS Indexes 
The system uses two separate FAISS indexes: one for job postings and one for resumes. This design 
supports bidirectional matching, allowing CVs to be matched to jobs and job postings to be matched 
to candidates. Separating the indexes improves clarity, efficiency and scalability of each workflow. 


--- PAGE 41 ---
 
34 
 
Deterministic Matching Design 
A deterministic approach was adopted for embedding generation and matching to ensure consistent 
outputs. Unlike generative AI systems, which may produce varying results for the same input, this 
approach guarantees that identical inputs produce identical outputs. This was particularly important 
given user feedback highlighting concerns about inconsistency in AI systems. 
Hybrid Scoring Approach 
Instead of relying solely on keyword matching or similarity scores, a hybrid scoring approach was 
implemented. Combining multiple recommendation approaches has been shown to improve system 
accuracy and relevance. (Deniz, 2021). This decision improved match accuracy and reduced the 
impact of misleading keyword matches. 
Explainability as a Core Design Principle 
Explainability is widely recognised as essential for improving user trust in AI-driven systems. 
(Wojciech Samek, 2017) Rather than returning only a match score, the system provides additional 
information, including matching skills, missing skills, and explanatory feedback. This improves 
transparency and allows users to better understand and act on the results.

5.16. Challenges Faced During Development 
Several challenges were encountered during system development, and addressing them led to 
significant improvements in the final implementation. 
The first challenge was related to data quality. Resume and job datasets often lacked consistent 
formatting and did not always present skills in a clearly structured way. This made early extraction 
and comparison less reliable. To address this, the preprocessing stage was improved, and a skill 
normalisation approach was introduced. FAISS indexes were also rebuilt when necessary to ensure 
consistency across stored vectors. 
The second challenge involved misleading keyword overlap. Early results showed that generic terms 
such as “communication”, “teamwork”, and “leadership” appeared frequently across many 
documents. Treating these skills equally to domain-specific skills led to inflated and unrealistic match 
scores. This issue was addressed by distinguishing between generic transferable skills and core 
domain skills and weighting them differently during scoring. 
The third challenge was that FAISS similarity retrieval alone was not sufficient. While FAISS returned 
the closest vectors efficiently, some of these results were still weak matches when examined in 
more detail. This led to the introduction of a hybrid matching approach, in which FAISS is used as the 
retrieval stage, with a second-stage scoring process applied to produce the final ranking. 
The fourth challenge was observed in recruiter-side matching. Recruiters typically expect stronger 
occupational relevance than general textual similarity. Some early results were too broad, 
particularly for roles such as marketing or service-based positions. To improve this, a recruiter-side 
re-ranking stage was introduced, allowing domain alignment and core skills to have a greater 
influence on the final score. 
The fifth challenge was ensuring consistency and explainability. A key requirement of the project 
was that the system should produce repeatable results and provide clear reasoning for match 
outcomes. To achieve this, the implementation used deterministic preprocessing, rule-based skill 
normalisation, fixed-length vector generation, and structured explanations based on matching and 
missing skills. This made the system outputs more stable and easier for users to understand.

7. Conclusions and Future Work 
 
7.1. Introduction 
This chapter concludes the AI Career Advisor project by outlining the main outcomes of the work, 
reviewing how well the first aims and objectives were achieved and identifying key limitations and 
areas for future improvement. 
The overall aim of the project was to design and implement an AI-powered career advisory platform 
that supports both job seekers and recruiters through intelligent matching, explainable feedback, 
and a dual-portal structure. Based on the final implementation and evaluation, this aim was 
achieved through the development of a working full-stack prototype that combines text processing, 
fixed-length vector representations, FAISS-based retrieval, hybrid scoring and user-facing 
explanation features. 
 
7.2. Summary of Achievements 
The project successfully developed a web-based AI Career Advisor system with a FastAPI backend, a 
Streamlit frontend, a SQLite database, and FAISS vector indexes. The final system supports two 
distinct user workflows. Job seekers can upload CVs and receive ranked job matches, along with 
matching skills, missing skills and explanation-based feedback. Recruiters can upload job 
descriptions and receive ranked candidate lists, supported by domain-aware re-ranking and 
recruiter-side query functionality. 
A key achievement of the project was the development of a structured matching pipeline rather 
than relying on a single method. The system processes text through preprocessing, skill extraction 
and normalisation and converts it into fixed-length vector representations. These vectors are then 
used for similarity retrieval using FAISS, followed by hybrid scoring to produce the final rankings. This 
approach yielded more balanced and meaningful outputs than simple keyword-based matching. 
Another important achievement was the focus on explainability. Instead of presenting only a 
similarity score, the system provides detailed feedback including matching skills, missing skills, and 
short textual explanations. This directly addresses one of the main limitations identified in existing 
recruitment systems: the lack of transparency for users. 
 
7.3. Key Findings 
One of the main findings from the project was that a hybrid scoring approach produced more 
effective results than using a single matching method. Keyword-based and TF-IDF style comparisons 
were able to identify basic overlap but were often too sensitive to wording and common generic 
skills. FAISS-based vector retrieval improved semantic matching and search efficiency. However, 
retrieval alone was not sufficient to produce reliable final rankings. 
The most effective results were achieved when vector retrieval was combined with additional 
factors, such as weighted skill overlap, missing-skill penalties, and recruiter-side re-ranking. This 
combination produced outputs that were more balanced and better aligned with realistic 
recruitment expectations for both job seekers and recruiters. 


--- PAGE 73 ---
 
66 
 
Another major finding was the importance of explainability. Providing matching skills, missing skills, 
and short explanations made the results easier to understand and more useful for users. This 
supports the idea that AI systems in recruitment should not just generate recommendations but also 
help users interpret and trust those results. 
A further finding was that recruiter-side matching requires a different level of precision compared to 
job seeker matching. For recruiters, domain relevance and core skills are more important than 
general similarity. Broad textual overlap alone was not always sufficient, prompting the introduction 
of recruiter-side re-ranking. This improvement increased the relevance of candidate 
recommendations and better reflected real-world recruitment needs. 
 
7.4. Limitations 
One limitation relates to the quality and structure of the dataset used. Some CVs and job 
descriptions contained inconsistent formatting, incomplete information, or noisy text, which 
affected the reliability of text extraction and skill identification. While preprocessing and 
normalisation steps improved the data quality, these issues were not fully removed. 
A second limitation is the use of a rule-based approach for skill extraction. This method delivers 
consistency and transparency, but it depends heavily on the coverage of the predefined skill 
mapping. As a result, some relevant skills may not be identified if they are expressed in less common 
ways or within more complex sentence structures. 
A further limitation is the use of a lightweight vector generation method. The system uses fixed-
length vector representations generated through a hashing-based approach rather than more 
advanced embedding models. While this supports efficient and deterministic matching, it may not 
capture deeper semantic relationships as effectively as more advanced techniques. 
The system continues to be a prototype and was developed within an academic context. It does not 
include integration with live job platforms, large-scale deployment infrastructure, or formal fairness 
and bias evaluation. Testing and evaluation were conducted in a controlled environment rather than 
a real-world recruitment setting, which may limit how well the results generalise in practice. 
 
7.5. Future Work 
There are several areas where the system could be further developed. One possible improvement is 
including live job data from external sources, such as public job APIs or recruitment platforms. This 
would make the system more realistic and increase its usefulness for real-life job searching, rather 
than relying only on static datasets. 
Another important extension would be the use of more advanced semantic text models. Replacing 
the current hashing-based vector generation method with a stronger embedding approach could 
improve the system’s ability to understand context and meaning. 
The skill extraction process could also be improved. Introducing more advanced NLP techniques 
would allow the system to recognise a wider range of terminology and improve the accuracy of 
extracted skills. 


--- PAGE 74 ---
 
67 
 
A further area for development is fairness and bias analysis. Since recruitment is a sensitive domain, 
future versions of the system should include methods for monitoring bias, evaluating outcomes, and 
supporting fairer decision-making. 
The recruiter-side assistant could also be expanded into a more advanced interactive feature. Future 
versions could allow recruiters to ask more detailed questions, compare multiple candidates, and 
generate structured summaries to support decision-making. 
The system could also be improved from a software engineering perspective. This includes migrating 
to a more scalable database such as PostgreSQL, deploying the system to a cloud environment and 
extending automated testing and monitoring. These improvements would support larger datasets 
and more realistic deployment scenarios. 
 
7.6. Conclusions 
In conclusion, this project demonstrated that it is possible to develop a practical AI-driven career-
advisory system that supports both job seekers and recruiters in a structured, transparent way. The 
final prototype successfully integrates a FastAPI backend, a Streamlit frontend, a SQLite database, 
and FAISS vector retrieval, alongside skill extraction, hybrid scoring, and explanation generation, to 
produce a functioning application that meets the main aims of the project. 
An important outcome of the project is the recognition that effective recruitment systems require 
more than automated matching. While improving efficiency is valuable, it is equally important that 
systems provide clear, understandable and user-centered feedback. By combining matching with 
explainability, the AI Career Advisor delivers a more transparent approach compared to many 
existing recruitment tools that focus primarily on automation.
